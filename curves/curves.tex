\chapter{Curves and curved surfaces}
\label{chap:curves}

The vast majority of geographic information uses only linear geometries (\ie\ line segments, polygons and polyhedra).
When curved geometries are present, they are usually simple parametric shapes, such as spheres, cylinders and cones in CSG or circular arcs in a few 2D datasets (Figure~\ref{fig:circulararc}).
Moreover, most data models for geographic data are designed with linear geometries in mind.

However, the real-world shapes that we find in the built environment are often curved, which means that representing them with linear geometries can only approximate them---and a good approximation might involve using many elements.
By contrast, working with representations for curved shapes can be both compact and more precise.

\begin{marginfigure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{figs/circulararc}
\caption{A composite curve made from two line segments and a circular arc. This curve could represent the corner of a pavement (sidewalk) or a turn where two roads cross.}%
\label{fig:circulararc}
\end{marginfigure}

Creating and manipulating fewer elements is also often simpler.
For instance, a user of 3D modelling or design software will find it easier to adjust the shape of a curved surface based on a few key points than to adjust the vertices of many small triangles to achieve a similar shape.
Most CAD and 3D modelling software thus support curves, and BIM models routinely use them internally as well.

Throughout this chapter, we will explain how to use curved (1D) lines and curved (2D) surfaces, which in turn can be used to model 3D objects as an extension of straight-line b-rep.
Among the possible methods that can be used for this purpose, this chapter covers two related methods that are often found in practice: B\'ezier curves/surfaces and b-splines.

\section{Background}

\subsection{Types of points}

In general, curve and curved surface modelling is done by specifying the locations of points, of which there are two kinds (Figure~\ref{subfig:pointtypesa}):

\label{fig:pointtypes}
\end{figure}

\begin{description}
\item[data points] are\marginnote{data point}\index{data point} points that the curve/surface needs to pass through; and
\item[control points] are\marginnote{control point}\index{control point} points that have some influence over the shape of the curve/surface, but through which the curve/surface does not necessarily pass. Intuitively, they `pull' the curve in their direction.
\end{description}

If you have some experience with vector-based graphic editors (\eg\ Adobe Illustrator, Inkscape or Sketch), you have likely drawn curves using data points and control points (Figure~\ref{subfig:pointtypesb}).

Note that often the distinction between the two kinds of points is not made and both are just referred to as control points, particularly in some contexts (\eg\ graphics software), whereas in others (\eg\ interpolation) the distinction is almost always made.
For the two representations in this chapter, descriptions of B\'ezier curves/surfaces usually distinguish between the two, whereas descriptions of b-splines usually do not.
We will follow this convention as well.

\subsection{Types of curves and surfaces}

There are three kinds of mathematical representations that are typically used to represent curves and curved surfaces.
From most restrictive to least restrictive, these are:

\begin{description}
\item[Explicit curves/surfaces] are\marginnote{explicit curve}\index{explicit curve}\marginnote{explicit surface}\index{explicit surface} modelled using a function that defines the value of one coordinate, generally \(y\) in 2D and \(z\) in 3D, based on the other coordinate(s).
For instance, \(f(x)=y=x^2\) can be used to define a parabola in 2D, and \(f(x,y)=z=x^2+y^2\) to define a paraboloid in 3D.
This makes it impossible to represent vertical lines in 2D and planes in 3D (without swapping the dependent and independent variables in the equation), and makes it difficult to have multiple values per dependent variable, with minor exceptions such as the use of plus or minus (\(\pm\)), \eg\ \(f(x)=y = \pm\sqrt{1-x^2}\) for a circle or \(f(x,y)=z = \pm\sqrt{1-x^2-y^2}\) for a sphere.

\item[Implicit curves/surfaces] are\marginnote{implicit curve}\index{implicit curve}\marginnote{implicit surface}\index{implicit surface} modelled using a single function with all coordinates as parameters.
For instance, \(f(x,y) = x^2+y^2=1\) defines a unit circle in 2D and \(f(x,y,z)=x^2+y^2+z^2=1\) defines a unit sphere in 3D.
This works fine with vertical lines/planes and can represent multiple values per dependent variable, but complete functions have to be built to represent different curves.

\item[Parametric curves/surfaces] are\marginnote{parametric curve}\index{parametric curve}\marginnote{parametric surface}\index{parametric surface} modelled using different functions per coordinate which have independent non-coordinate variables as \emph{parameters}.
These functions are evaluated at a range of values, which is known as the \emph{parameter space}\marginnote{parameter space}\index{parameter space}.
For instance, \(f(x,y) = (\cos t, \sin t)\) where the parameter \(t\) is evaluated at \(0 \leq t \leq 2\pi \) defines a unit circle in 2D.
Similarly, \(f(x,y,z) = (\cos \theta \sin \phi , \sin \theta \sin \phi, \cos \phi)\) with \(0 \leq \theta \leq 2\pi \) and \(0 \leq \phi \leq \pi \) defines a unit sphere in 3D.
This is the most flexible approach because it allows us to define curves and surfaces in a general form that works independently of the coordinates used. 
\end{description}

Because of their flexibility, most curve modelling methods are based on parametric curves/surfaces.
For the rest of this chapter, we will therefore be working with parametric curves/surfaces only.

\subsection{Tangent vectors and other derivatives}

If we compute the first derivative of a parametric curve with respect to its parameter(s) at a given point, we get vector(s)\marginnote{tangent vector}\index{tangent vector} with a direction that is tangent to the curve and a magnitude that tells us the rate of change of the parameter(s) at that point.
For instance, in the curve \(C(x,y) = (\cos t, \sin t)\) with \(0 \leq t \leq 2\pi \) (Figure~\ref{fig:derivatives}), which describes the unit circle, at \(t=0\) we get the point \((1, 0)\), \ie\ the rightmost point on the circle using the typical axis directions.
The tangent vector is then \(dC(x,y)/dt = (-\sin t, \cos t)\), which at \(t=0\) is \((0, 1)\), \ie\ a unit vector pointing upwards (which makes sense considering that it draws the circle in a counter-clockwise direction).

\begin{marginfigure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figs/derivatives}
\caption[A parametric curve and its derivatives]{The parametric curve \(C(x,y) = (\cos t, \sin t)\) for the unit circle (black), its first derivative at \(t=0\) (green), and the second derivative (red).}%
\label{fig:derivatives}
\end{marginfigure}

A common analogy to understand this concept is to consider a moving particle, which you could find in an animation showing the current point along a path.
Since this particle moves as time passes, you will often see that the parameter \(t\) used in parametric functions.
To recap: the parametric function tells us the position of the particle at any given time, and the tangent vector tells us the direction and speed of the particle at that time.

The second derivative of a parametric curve is a little harder to visualise, but it is also a vector that tells us the rate of change of the curvature at a point.
In the particle analogy, it is the acceleration of the particle (and its acceleration direction).
In the circle from the previous example, the second derivative is \(d^2C(x,y)/dt^2 = (-\cos t, -\sin t)\), which at \(t=0\) is \((-1, 0)\), \ie\ a unit vector towards the centre of the circle.

\subsection{Continuity}

In order to describe how multiple curves or surfaces should be joined, we rely on the concept of continuity, of which there are two types: geometric and parametric.
Geometric continuity\marginnote{geometric continuity}\index{geometric continuity} can be defined as follows:

\begin{description}
\item[Positional] (\(G^0\)) continuity\marginnote{positional continuity}\index{positional continuity}\marginnote{\(G^0\) continuity}\index{\(G^0\) continuity} means that the boundary of a curve (ie a point) or surface (ie a curve) matches that of its neighbours, \ie\ there are no gaps at common boundaries (Figure~\ref{subfig:g0});
\item[Tangential] (\(G^1\))\marginnote{tangential continuity}\index{tangential continuity}\marginnote{\(G^1\) continuity}\index{\(G^1\) continuity} means that the angles of curves or surfaces match those of its neighbours at their common boundaries, \ie\ no sharp edges (creases / folds) at common boundaries (Figure~\ref{subfig:g1});
\item[Curvature] (\(G^2\))\marginnote{curvature continuity}\index{curvature continuity}\marginnote{\(G^2\) continuity}\index{\(G^2\) continuity} means that the curvature of a curve or surface matches that of its neighbours at their common boundaries, \ie\ not even `soft' edges at common boundaries.
\end{description}

\centering
\begin{subfigure}{0.4\linewidth}

\caption{}%
\label{subfig:g0}

\label{fig:g}
\end{figure}

Generalising from here, we can say that a curve has \(G^n\) continuity\marginnote{\(G^n\) continuity}\index{\(G^n\) continuity} at a boundary point when the \(n\)-th derivatives have the same direction at that point.
If they have the same magnitude as well, it is also said to have \(C^n\) continuity.
Therefore, \(C^n\) continuity\marginnote{\(C^n\) continuity}\index{\(C^n\) continuity} implies \(G^n\) continuity.

Curves and surfaces with \(C^n\) continuity look the same as their \(G^n\) counterparts.
However, the distinction is important in applications that depend on more subtle geometric properties of curves.
For example, if we put a texture (ie an image) on a set of surfaces with only 
\(G_1\) continuity, the texture will seem to stretch differently on different surfaces.
Simulations of different kinds, such as with fluids or building stresses also often require \(C^n\) continuity.

\section{B\'ezier curves}

B\'ezier curves\marginnote{B\'ezier curve}\index{B\'ezier curve} are parametric curves that are based on a polynomial function with one parameter.
They are named after Pierre B\'ezier, who developed them to model the stylised shapes of cars while working at Renault in the 1960s.
Interestingly enough, they were also independently developed by Paul de Casteljau at Citro\"en, likely before B\'ezier, but it appears that he was not allowed to publish them.
However, De Casteljau's algorithm (Section~\ref{sec:decasteljau}), which is used to evaluate B\'ezier curves, is named after him.

\subsection{A single B\'ezier segment}

Given a sequence of points, the B\'ezier curve starts from the first point and ends at the last point, whereas the intermediate points are treated as control points that `pull' the curve in their direction, but always remaining inside the convex hull of the points.
This is known as a \emph{B\'ezier segment}\marginnote{B\'ezier segment}\index{B\'ezier segment}.

The tangent vector at the first point points to the second point, whereas the tangent vector at the last point points from the next-to-last point to it.
Similar constructions can be made for the higher derivatives, with the \(n\)-th derivative being determined only by \(n+1\) points.

If there are no intermediate points, the result is a linear B\'ezier curve\marginnote{linear B\'ezier curve}\index{linear B\'ezier curve}, which is equivalent to a straight line between the two endpoints.
The most common forms of B\'ezier curves are however quadratic B\'ezier curves\marginnote{quadratic B\'ezier curve}\index{quadratic B\'ezier curve} (Figure~\ref{fig:quadratic}), which have one intermediate point, \eg\ \(p_{\mathrm{quadratic}} = (p_0, p_1, p_2)\), and cubic B\'ezier curves (Figure~\ref{fig:cubic}), which have two intermediate points, \eg\ \(p_{\mathrm{cubic}} = (p_0, p_1, p_2, p_3)\).
Note the tangent vectors in both figures, as well as how the curves always fit within the convex hull of the points.

A B\'ezier curve \(C\) can be formulated as a sort of weighted average of its points \((p_i, \ldots, p_n)\):

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:beziercurve}
C(t) = \sum_{i=0}^n B^n_i(t) p_i\text{, for }0 \leq t \leq 1
\end{equation}

where \(B^n_i\) is the weight associated with the point \(p_i\).

Intuitively, you can imagine that we want this weight to reach a maximum when we are close to the point and decrease as we move farther from it.
For example, in a quadratic B\'ezier curve, \(B^n_0\) should start from its maximum value at \(t=0\) and decrease as \(t\) increases, \(B^n_1\) should start low, increase to reach a maximum at \(t=0.5\) and decrease afterwards, and \(B^n_2\) should start from its lowest point and increase to reach its maximum at \(t=1\).

The exact functions used to determine the weights in B\'ezier curves are called \emph{Bernstein polynomials}\marginnote{Bernstein polynomials}\index{Bernstein polynomials} (Figure~\ref{fig:bernstein}), named after Sergei Natanovich Bernstein who discovered them in the 1910s.
These are given by:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bernstein}
B^n_i(t) = \binom{n}{i} t^i (1-t)^{n-i}\text{, where }\binom{n}{i} = \frac{n!}{i! (n-i)!}
\end{equation}

where \(n\) is the degree of the B\'ezier curve, \ie\ 1 for linear, 2 for quadratic, 3 for cubic, etc.
\(\binom{n}{i}\) is the \emph{binomial coefficient}\marginnote{binomial coefficient}\index{binomial coefficient}, which is equivalent to the \(i\)-th column of the \(n\)-th row in Pascal's triangle\marginnote{Pascal's triangle}\index{Pascal's triangle} (Figure~\ref{fig:pascal}).

Linear B\'ezier curves (\(n = 1\)) are thus given by:

\begin{align}
\label{eq:bezier1}
C(t) &= \sum_{i=0}^1 B^1_i(t) p_i \nonumber \\
&= (1-t) p_0 + t p_1\text{, for }0 \leq t \leq 1.
\end{align}

which is equivalent to the parametric equation of the line we discussed in the background.
Quadratic B\'ezier curves (\(n = 2\)) are given by:

\begin{align}
\label{eq:bezier2}
C(t) &= \sum_{i=0}^2 B^2_i(t) p_i \nonumber \\
&= (1-t)^2 p_0 + 2 t (1-t) p_1 + t^2 p_2\text{, for }0 \leq t \leq 1.
\end{align}

And cubic B\'ezier curves (\(n = 3\)) are given by:

\begin{small}
\begin{align}
\label{eq:bezier3}
C(t) &= \sum_{i=0}^3 B^3_i(t) p_i \nonumber \\
&= (1-t)^3 p_0 + 3 t (1-t)^2 p_1 + 3 t^2 (1-t) p_2 + t^3 p_3\text{, for }0 \leq t \leq 1.
\end{align}
\end{small}

\subsection{De Casteljau's algorithm}
\label{sec:decasteljau}

While the Bernstein polynomial form (Equation~\ref{eq:beziercurve}) is useful for analysis, evaluating a B\'ezier curve using De Casteljau's algorithm\marginnote{De Casteljau's algorithm}\index{De Casteljau's algorithm} provides a simple geometric construction that makes the behaviour of the curve more intuitive.

Given a set of points \((p_0, \ldots, p_n)\) defining a B\'ezier segment of degree \(n\) and a parameter value \(t\), the algorithm proceeds by repeatedly performing linear interpolation between consecutive points:

\begin{align}
p_i^{0} &= p_i \nonumber \\
p_i^{j} &= (1-t) p_i^{j-1} + t p_{i+1}^{j-1}
\end{align}

where \(1 \leq j \leq n\) and \(0 \leq i \leq n-j\).
At each level \(j\), we obtain one fewer point than at the previous level.
After \(n\) levels, we are left with a single point \(p_0^{n}\), which is the point on the B\'ezier curve at parameter \(t\).

The intermediate points also have geometric meaning.
For a quadratic B\'ezier curve \((p_0, p_1, p_2)\) at \(t=0.5\), the first level produces the midpoints \(p_0^1\) of \(p_0p_1\) and \(p_1^1\) of \(p_1p_2\), and the second level produces the midpoint \(p_0^2\) of their segment, which lies on the curve.
More generally, the vector from \(p_1^{n-1}\) to \(p_0^{n-1}\) at any \(t\) gives the direction of the tangent vector at that point.

This algorithm is numerically stable and is often used in practice to render B\'ezier curves by recursively subdividing them until the segments are small enough to be approximated as straight lines (eg a single pixel segment in a computer).
Indeed, it was developed by Paul de Casteljau at Citro\"en for precisely this purpose.

\subsection{Composite B\'ezier curves}

In the examples before, we saw how low-degree B\'ezier segments (quadratic, cubic) can be used to model simple curves.
In theory, they can also be used to model arbitrarily complex ones as well (with plenty of turns) by using high-degree polynomials instead, which is equivalent to curves with a lot of control points.
However, in a B\'ezier curve, all control points affect all parts of the curve.
In other words, moving a control point slightly will have effects everywhere on the curve, which in turn makes it difficult to control its shape.

% About non Bezier polynomials, better to skip
% For instance, in 2D, a polynomial of degree one (\ie\ a straight line) is a linear function of the form \(f(t) = at+b\) that can be defined so as to pass through two points, a polynomial of degree two (\ie\ a parabola) is a quadratic function of the form \(f(t)=at^2+bt+c\) that can be made to pass through three points, a polynomial of degree three is a cubic function of the form \(f(t)=at^3+bt^2+ct\) that can be made to pass through four points, and so on.
% We can therefore use a polynomial of degree \(n\) to model a curve passing through \(n+1\) points.
% However, high-degree polynomials wobble uncontrollably to pass through all the points, and a small change in the position of one of the points can cause large changes all over the curve.
% Therefore, we typically don't want them because they make it difficult to control the shape of the resulting curve or surface.

Therefore, when we want to model a curve or curved surface with a complex shape with many bends, the better approach is to split it into \emph{segments}\marginnote{curve segment}\index{curve segment} (for curves) and \emph{patches}\marginnote{surface patch}\index{surface patch} (for surfaces).
In this way, we can model each part independently and get more precise results.

With B\'ezier curves, we therefore join multiple low-degree B\'ezier segments (usually cubic) together into a \emph{composite B\'ezier curve}\marginnote{composite B\'ezier curve}\index{composite B\'ezier curve} or a \emph{polybezier}\marginnote{polybezier}\index{polybezier} (accent usually omitted).
When they are joined in a loop, \ie\ joining the last to the first, it is sometimes called a \emph{beziergon}\marginnote{beziergon}\index{beziergon} or \emph{bezigon}\marginnote{bezigon}\index{bezigon}.
Many common vector file formats use these, including those used in fonts, PDF files, and SVG images.

Connecting multiple B\'ezier segments with \(G_0\) or \(C_0\) continuity simply means that their common endpoint should be the same.
That is, if we have a composite B\'ezier curve formed by two adjacent B\'ezier segments, where the first is defined by the points \((p_0, \ldots, p_n)\) and the second is defined by the points \((q_0, \ldots, q_n)\), we need to enforce that \(p_n = q_0\).

As we previously discussed, the tangent vector of the endpoint of a B\'ezier curve is related only to the endpoint and its neighbour.
Therefore, \(G_1\) continuity can be achieved by making sure that the common endpoint and its two neighbours, \ie\ \(p_{n-1}\) and \(q_1\), are collinear (Figure~\ref{fig:polybezier}).
For \(C_1\) continuity, they should also be evenly spaced.
\(G_2\) and \(C_2\) continuity is more difficult to explain intuitively, but it basically involves mirrored configurations with more control points on both neighbours.

\section{B\'ezier surfaces}
\subsection{Rectangular B\'ezier surfaces}

Moving on to 3D (through b-rep), the most common implementation of B\'ezier surfaces uses rectangular patches, which are made of grids of points.
The most common are biquadratic\marginnote{biquadratic B\'ezier surface}\index{biquadratic B\'ezier surface} (\(3 \times 3\); Figure~\ref{fig:quadraticsurf}) and bicubic\marginnote{bicubic B\'ezier surface}\index{bicubic B\'ezier surface} (\(4 \times 4\); Figure~\ref{fig:cubicsurf}) surfaces, which are defined based on square matrices of points, such as:

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figs/quadraticsurf}
\caption[A B\'ezier biquadratic surface and the points that define it]{A B\'ezier biquadratic surface and the points that define it.
Note how the four corners are the only data points and how the surface is tangent to them.}%
\label{fig:quadraticsurf}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figs/cubicsurf}
\caption[A B\'ezier bicubic surface and the points that define it]{A B\'ezier bicubic surface and the points that define it.
Note how the four corners are the only data points and how the surface is tangent to them.}%
\label{fig:cubicsurf}
\end{figure}

\begin{align}
p_{\mathrm{biquadratic}} &= \left(\begin{array}{ccc}
p_{0,0} & p_{0,1} & p_{0,2} \\
p_{1,0} & p_{1,1} & p_{1,2} \\
p_{2,0} & p_{2,1} & p_{2,2} 
\end{array}\right)\text{, and } \\
p_{\mathrm{bicubic}} &= \left(\begin{array}{cccc}
p_{0,0} & p_{0,1} & p_{0,2} & p_{0,3} \\
p_{1,0} & p_{1,1} & p_{1,2} & p_{1,3} \\
p_{2,0} & p_{2,1} & p_{2,2} & p_{2,3} \\
p_{3,0} & p_{3,1} & p_{3,2} & p_{3,3}
\end{array}\right),
\end{align}

where only the four corner points are data points and all the others are control points.
Note that the four sides of a B\'ezier surface are B\'ezier curves using the points on the top/bottom/left/right of the matrix.

A rectangular B\'ezier surface is described as:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bezierrectangle}
S(u, v) = \sum_{i=0}^n \sum_{j=0}^m B^n_i(u) B^m_j(v) p_{i,j}\text{, for }0 \leq u \leq 1, 0 \leq v \leq 1.
\end{equation}

where \(p_{i,j}\) is a point in an \(m \times n\) matrix that defines the data points and control points used for the patch.

As with composite B\'ezier curves, \emph{composite B\'ezier surfaces}\marginnote{composite B\'ezier surface}\index{composite B\'ezier surface} can be created by joining together multiple rectangular B\'ezier patches\marginnote{B\'ezier patch}\index{B\'ezier patch}.
These follow the same logic as the composite B\'ezier curves.

In order to get \(G_0\) and \(C_0\) continuity, the common points at the boundary of the two matrices should be the same.
That is, if we have a composite B\'ezier surface formed by two adjacent B\'ezier rectangular patches, where the first is defined by the matrix \(p\) and the second by the matrix \(q\), which are defined as:

\begin{equation}
p = \left(\begin{array}{ccc}
p_{0,0} & \cdots & p_{0,n} \\
\vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
p_{m,0} & \cdots & p_{m,n} 
\end{array}\right),
q = \left(\begin{array}{ccc}
q_{0,0} & \cdots & q_{0,n} \\
\vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
q_{m,0} & \cdots & q_{m,n} 
\end{array}\right),
\end{equation}

and they are joined at the curve defined by \(p_{i,n}\) and \(q_{i,0}\), for \(0 \leq i \leq n\), we simply need to enforce that \(p_{i,n} = q_{i, 0}\).

For \(G_1\) continuity, we need to ensure that the tangent vector at the common curve has the same direction, which is given by:

\begin{equation}
\left. \frac{\partial p(u,v)}{\partial v} \right|_{v=1} = a \left. \frac{\partial q(u,v)}{\partial v} \right|_{v=0}.
\end{equation}

where \(a\) can have any positive value.
For \(C_1\) continuity, the magnitude of the vector needs to be the same, which means that \(a = 1\) in the previous equation.
Just as with composite B\'ezier curves, these conditions are achieved when each point along the common boundary curve and its neighbours on either side patch.
That is, for all \(i\), \(p_{i, n-1}\), \(q_{i, 0}\) and \(q_{i, 1}\), are collinear (for \(G_1\)) and also evenly spaced (for \(C_1\)).

These are better parametrised in terms of three barycentric coordinates, which we here denote as \(u\), \(v\) and \(w\) (Figure~\ref{fig:triangle}).

Triangular B\'ezier surfaces are defined based on triangular arrangements of points of the form:

B^n_{i,j,k}(u,v,w) = \frac{n!}{i!j!k!}u^i v^j w^k

In order to create a composite B\'ezier surface from triangular B\'ezier patches, we need to have similar constraints as before.
For \(G_0\) and \(C_0\) continuity, the common boundary points should be the same.
That is, if we have a composite B\'ezier surface formed by two adjacent B\'ezier triangular patches, where the first is defined by the matrix \(p\) and the second by the matrix \(q\), which are defined as:

\begin{equation}
p = \begin{array}{cccccc}
&&&& \multicolumn{2}{c}{\multirow{2}{*}{\(p_{0,n,0}\)}} \\
&& \multicolumn{2}{c}{\multirow{2}{*}{\reflectbox{\(\ddots \)}}} \\
\multicolumn{2}{c}{\multirow{2}{*}{\(p_{n,0,0}\)}} &&& \multicolumn{2}{c}{\multirow{2}{*}{\(\vdots \)}} \\
&& \multicolumn{2}{c}{\multirow{2}{*}{\(\ddots \)}} \\
&&&& \multicolumn{2}{c}{\multirow{2}{*}{\(p_{0,0,n}\)}} \\
\\
\end{array},
q = \begin{array}{cccccc}
\multicolumn{2}{c}{\multirow{2}{*}{\(q_{0,n,0}\)}} \\
&& \multicolumn{2}{c}{\multirow{2}{*}{\(\ddots \)}} \\
\multicolumn{2}{c}{\multirow{2}{*}{\(\vdots \)}} &&& \multicolumn{2}{c}{\multirow{2}{*}{\(q_{n,0,0}\)}} \\
&& \multicolumn{2}{c}{\multirow{2}{*}{\reflectbox{\(\ddots \)}}} \\
\multicolumn{2}{c}{\multirow{2}{*}{\(q_{0,0,n}\)}} \\
\\
\end{array},
\end{equation}

and they are joined at the curve defined by \(u=0\) on both, \ie\ \(p_{0,j,n-j}\) and \(q_{0,j,n-j}\), for all \(0 \leq j \leq n\), we need to enforce that \(p_{0,j,n-j} = q_{0,j,n-j}\).

For \(G_1\) continuity, it is somewhat more complex than for rectangular B\'ezier surfaces.
Basically, we need to ensure that along the common boundary curve, three specific vectors starting at every common point on the boundary surface except for the last one, \ie\ \(p_{0,j,n-j}\), for all \(0 \leq j < n\), should be coplanar.
These three vectors are the ones pointing to: (i) the next point along the common curve (\ie\ \(p_{0, j, n-j}\) to \(p_{0, j+1, n-j-1}\)), (ii) the next point in the \(u\) direction in the patch on the left (\ie\ \(p_{0, j, n-j}\) to \(p_{1, j, n-j-1}\)), and (iii) the next point in the \(u\) direction in the patch on the right (\ie\ \(q_{0, j, n-j}\) to \(q_{1, j, n-j-1}\)).

\section{B-splines and NURBS}

\subsection{B-splines}

Spline curves are named after \emph{flat splines}\marginnote{flat spline}\index{flat spline}, which were thin flexible strips of wood or metal used to draw smooth curves before the advent of computers (Figure~\ref{fig:spline}).
Out of the many varieties of spline curves, we are most interested in \emph{basis splines}, or \emph{b-splines}\marginnote{b-spline}\index{b-spline} for short, which have a number of properties that make them very useful for modelling curves.

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.3\linewidth]{figs/spline}
\caption{Splines are named after the flexible pieces of wood that were used to draw curves, often used for engineering drawings before computers. From Wikipedia Commons.}%
\label{fig:spline}
\end{figure}

A b-spline curve is a parametric curve created based on two kinds of elements: (i) a sequence of control points that are used by the segments in the curve, and (ii) a sequence of non-decreasing values called \emph{knots}\marginnote{knot}\index{knot}, which define how the parameters in the parametric curve are evaluated.
The control points are similar to the data and control points used in B\'ezier curves.
The knots are the values of the parameter that split the curve into segments, which in the case of a simple B\'ezier curve (which is uniform) were simply 0 and 1 (which are the end points of the curve).

The sequence of knots, usually known as the \emph{knot vector}\marginnote{knot vector}\index{knot vector}, also describes the control points that affect each segment of the curve.
Since only some control points affect each segment, their shape is easier to control,  which means that we don't have to simply it into smaller segments to describe more complex shapes.

Let us go through a more precise definition of a b-spline and its knots.
Given a sequence of \(n+1\) control points \((p_0, \ldots, p_n)\) and a knot vector 
\((t_0, \ldots, t_m)\), a b-spline curve of degree \(k\) is a weighted average of its control points, which is given by:

\begin{equation}
C(t) = \sum_{i=0}^{n} B_{i,k}(t) p_i
\end{equation}

where \(B_{i,k}(t)\) are the b-spline basis functions of degree \(k\), which are defined recursively using the Cox--de Boor recursion formula\marginnote{Cox--de Boor recursion}\index{Cox--de Boor recursion} as:

\begin{align}
B_{i,0}(t) &= \begin{cases}
1 & \text{if } t_i  \leq t < t_{i+1} \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases} \\
B_{i,k}(t) &= \frac{t-t_i}{t_{i+k}-t_i} B_{i,p-1}(t) + \frac{t_{i+k+1}-t}{t_{i+k+1}-t_{i+1}}B_{i+1,k-1}(t)
\end{align}

Note how the \(B_{i,k}(t)\) basis functions act as the weights of the weighted average, which is similar to the role of the Bernstein polynomials in B\'ezier curves.
However, unlike the Bernstein polynomials, the weights of the basis functions only have a value above zero within a limited range (Figure~\ref{fig:bspline}).
This relates to how each segment of a b-spline is only affected by some control points (ie those with a value above 0).
This means that adjusting the location of a control point will only have a local effect, which is known as \emph{local control}\marginnote{local control}\index{local control}.
Continuity in b-splines is also built-in, where a b-spline of degree \(k\) has \(C^{k-1}\) continuity everywhere, including at its knots.

\subsection{Uniform vs. non-uniform curves}

As we saw previously, in a parametric curve \(C(t)\), specific values of \(t\) represent points that are used to split the curve into segments.
When these values of \(t\) are evenly spaced, we devote the same amount of parameter space for each segment and we call it a \emph{uniform} parametric curve\marginnote{uniform parametric curve}\index{uniform parametric curve} (Figure~\ref{fig:segments}).

\begin{figure*}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figs/segments}
\caption{A composite B\'ezier curve made from three segments. In composite B\'ezier curves, the values of \(t\) are evenly spaced, and it is therefore a uniform parametric curve.}%
\label{fig:segments}
\end{figure*}

Similarly, in a parametric surface \(S(u, v)\), specific values of \(u\) are curves on the surface, as are values of \(v\).
A set of two curves with fixed \(u\) and two curves with fixed \(v\) will bound a patch, \eg\ \(S(u, 0)\), \(S(u, 1)\), \(S(0, v)\) and \(S(1, v)\) (Figure~\ref{fig:patch}).
If the values of both \(u\) and \(v\) are both evenly spaced, we devote the same amount of parameter space for each patch, which makes it a \emph{uniform} parametric surface\marginnote{uniform parametric surface}\index{uniform parametric surface}.

\begin{figure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{figs/patch}
\caption{A B\'ezier rectangular patch.}%
\label{fig:patch}
\end{figure}

However, it is important to note that using the same amount of parameter space to each segment/patch does not mean the same geometric distance.
In other words, even if we have the moving particle moving from \(t = 0\) to \(t = 1\) at a constant speed, we will likely not move at the same speed across the curve or surface (with the exception of some very specific curves).
This is easiest to see when we consider that the data or control points are not evenly spaced either---we will move slower through parts with bunched up points, but it will also happen even when the points are evenly spaced.

When we use different amounts of parameter space per segment or patch, we can define a \emph{non-uniform} parametric curve\marginnote{non-uniform parametric curve}\index{non-uniform parametric curve} or \emph{non-uniform} parametric surface\marginnote{non-uniform parametric surface}\index{non-uniform parametric surface}.
These are more powerful than their uniform counterparts, meaning that we can better represent certain shapes (eg exact circles or sharp corners) and have better control of the resulting shape with fewer control points.

Returning to the specific case of splines, in \emph{uniform} b-splines\marginnote{uniform b-spline}\index{uniform b-spline}, the knot values are evenly spaced, \eg\ \((0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5)\).

A b-spline segment can therefore be expressed using a basis matrix:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bsplinesegment}
B_i(t) = \left(\begin{array}{ccc}t^{k-1} & \cdots & t^0\end{array} \right) M_k \left(\begin{array}{c} p_i \\ \vdots \\ p_{i+k-1} \end{array}\right)
\end{equation}

where \(M_k\) is the \(k \times k\) basis matrix for order \(k\).
For the first few values of \(k\), these are:

\begin{equation*}
\label{eq:bsplinematrices}
M_2 = \left(\begin{array}{cc}-1 & 1 \\
1 & 0\end{array}\right),\quad
M_3 = \frac{1}{2}\left(\begin{array}{ccc}1 & -2 & 1 \\
-2 & 2 & 0 \\
1 & 1 & 0\end{array}\right),\quad
M_4 = \frac{1}{6}\left(\begin{array}{cccc}-1 & 3 & -3 & 1 \\
3 & -6 & 3 & 0 \\

\end{equation*}

corresponding to linear (\(k=2\)), quadratic (\(k=3\)), and cubic (\(k=4\)) uniform b-spline segments.

\subsection{NURBS}

\emph{Non-uniform rational b-splines}\marginnote{NURBS}\index{NURBS}, commonly abbreviated as NURBS, are a generalisation of b-splines that adds two important capabilities: non-uniform knots and rational weights.

In a \emph{non-uniform} b-spline\marginnote{non-uniform}\index{non-uniform b-spline}, the knot vector can have uneven spacing, and individual knots can be repeated (multiplicity\marginnote{knot multiplicity}\index{knot multiplicity}).
Repeating a knot reduces the continuity at that point: a knot with multiplicity \(m\) reduces the continuity from \(C^{p-1}\) to \(C^{p-m}\).
This makes it possible to create sharp corners or even discontinuities within the same spline, which is essential for modelling complex shapes.
Non-uniform knots also allow for better parametrisation when the control points are unevenly spaced relative to the shape of the curve.

The \emph{rational} aspect of NURBS\marginnote{rational curve}\index{rational curve} means that each control point is assigned a scalar weight \(w_i\), making the curve a rational function (a ratio of two polynomials).
The NURBS curve is defined as:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:nurbscurve}
C(t) = \frac{\sum_{i=0}^n N_{i,p}(t) w_i p_i}{\sum_{i=0}^n N_{i,p}(t) w_i}
\end{equation}

where \(N_{i,p}(t)\) are the standard b-spline basis functions.
The denominator is a scalar that normalises the weighted sum, ensuring that the curve is invariant under affine transformations (translation, rotation, scaling).

The weights give additional control over the shape: increasing the weight of a control point `pulls' the curve closer to it, while decreasing it pushes the curve away.
When all weights are equal (conventionally set to 1), the NURBS curve reduces to a standard (non-rational) b-spline.

One of the most important properties of NURBS is their ability to represent \emph{conic sections}\marginnote{conic section}\index{conic section} (circles, ellipses, parabolas, and hyperbolas) exactly.
This is impossible with polynomial B\'ezier curves or uniform b-splines, which can only approximate these shapes.
For example, a full circle can be represented exactly using a NURBS curve of degree 2 with seven control points and a carefully chosen knot vector with rational weights.

NURBS have become the industry standard for curve and surface representation in computer-aided design and manufacturing.
They are used in data exchange formats such as IGES and STEP, and are supported by virtually all major CAD software packages (\eg\ SolidWorks, CATIA, AutoCAD, Rhinoceros 3D).
Importantly, NURBS subsume both B\'ezier curves and uniform b-splines: a B\'ezier curve is a special case of a NURBS curve where the knot vector has full multiplicity at the endpoints (e.g.\ \((0,0,\ldots,0,1,1,\ldots,1)\)) and all weights are equal.
